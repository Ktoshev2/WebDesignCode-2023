My """"Genius"""": The Piano

I have been playing the piano for about five years now.

As a classical pianist focused on late Romance music,

most of what I learn and perform was written by the three people below:

Frederick Chopin (1810-1849). Born in Zelazowa Wola, Poland, died in Paris, France.

Some of his pieces that I have performed include his Ballade in F major, Revolutionary Etude, and his Minute Waltz

Sergei Rachmaninoff (1873-1943). Born in Starorussky Uyezd, Russia, died Beverly Hills, CA.

Some of his pieces that I have performed include his Prelude in C Sharp Minor, and his Etude-Tableaux No. 4

Sergei Rachmaninoff (1875-1937). Born in Ciboure, France, died Paris, France.

Some of his pieces that I have performed include his À la manière de Borodine/Chabrier, Le Tombeau de Couperin, and Ondine

How do I learn and practice pieces?

Chopin's Ballade No. 2 as an example:


The first step for me when I learn a piece is to listen to it whilst

following along on the sheet music. When listening, I break the piece up into

stylistic or technical sections distinct from each other.


Once I have all sections divided, I count and number them. The hardest sections

have the lowest numbers, and the easiest sections have the highest numbers.

I then allot a certain amount of minutes per practice session to each section according

to its number.


From there, I have three main methods of actually practicing the music.


Using a Metronome

The first method consists of using a metronome, obviously, to keep a steady tempo when drilling

through unfamiliar music. Once I become proficient in mundane technical work, I move to the second method:


Slow, Soft, and Melodic Practice

Being capable of the basic technique of a section of the piece allows me to begin to focus on the

interpretation and musicality of it. Nobody likes to hear a robot bash on the keyboard, after all.

To further increase my technical proficiency, I do this while playing the section slowly, and as soft as possible.

The key here is to realize that playing softly actually requires far more technical capability and skill than

playing loudly, as it requires more control of the hands. Once I can comfortably run through the entire piece like this, I move to

the final method:


The Pencil Method

The "pencil method" first entails grabbing a pile of 2 to 10 pencils and putting them on the left side of my piano.

Then, I run through a given section of a piece. If I am satisfied by my performance and cannot find any noticeable flaws,

I move one pencil to the right side of the piano. If I am not satisfied, I move one pencil back to the left side.

Finally, I continue playing the section over and over again until all pencils are on the right side of my piano.

A potential flaw in this method arises from not scrutinizing the playing deeeply enough, and so I make sure to be as

musically brutal as possible in the analysis of my performance.


Example Practice Routine: Ballade No. 2 in F Major


Section Practice Method
Beginning Pencil method: 2 pencils
First A minor arpeggios Pencil method: 4 pencils
G minor arpeggios Soft, slow, melodic: 6 minutes
Transition into climax and cooldown Pencil method: 3 pencils
Second slow part Pencil method: 2 pencils
First chords/third slow part Pencil method: 2 pencils
Second chords Pencil method: 2 pencils
D minor arpeggios Soft, slow, melodic: 6 minutes
Second A minor arpeggios Pencil method: 3 pencils
Coda, first half Pencil method: 3 pencils
Coda, second half Pencil method: 3 pencils
Finale Pencil method: 5 pencils
Ease out Pencil method: 2 pencils
